    \textbf{Abstract} \\[0.3cm]

\end{center}

\normalsize

Knowledge-graph-aware (KG-aware) recommenders work well on dense KGs, but sparse KGs are underexplored. On a sparse review-derived KG ($2.4$ aspect edges per item), four representative methods (KGAT, KGCL, MCCLK, KGRec) are all outperformed by pure LightGCN: each embeds the KG into the scoring pipeline with no structural switch to disengage it when unreliable, letting KG noise contaminate the collaborative gradient.

We propose RA-GARK (Rationale-Aware Gating over Review-Aspect Knowledge graphs), which treats the KG as a gated side channel that starts closed, so training begins as pure LightGCN, and opens only where gradients show the KG improves the collaborative signal, giving structural graceful degradation. Under a fixed KG, RA-GARK reaches NDCG@20 $= 0.1238$, beating KGRec by $+13.1\%$ and pure LightGCN by $+5.0\%$, turning the KG's contribution from net-negative into net-positive without altering the KG itself. These results, obtained on a single sparse benchmark, suggest that gating the KG as a side channel is an effective design principle when the KG is sparse or unreliable.\\[0.5\baselineskip]
\textbf{Keywords: knowledge-graph-aware recommendation; graceful degradation; fusion gate; sparse knowledge graph; collaborative filtering.}
