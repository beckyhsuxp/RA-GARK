    \textbf{Abstract} \\[0.3cm]

\end{center}

\normalsize

Knowledge-graph-aware (KG-aware) recommenders work well on dense KGs, but sparse KGs are underexplored. On a sparse review-derived KG ($2.4$ aspect edges per item), four representative methods (KGAT, KGCL, MCCLK, KGRec) are all outperformed by pure LightGCN: each embeds the KG into the scoring pipeline with no structural switch to disengage it when unreliable, letting KG noise contaminate the collaborative gradient.

We propose RA-GARK (Rationale-Aware Gating over Review-Aspect Knowledge graphs), which treats the KG as a gated side channel rather than a mandatory pipeline component, realized through three design choices. First, a local-biased fusion gate starts closed, so training begins as pure LightGCN, and opens only where gradients show the KG improves the collaborative signal, giving structural graceful degradation. Second, a softmax aspect-saliency attention summarizes each item's KG semantics into a few latent aspect slots and selects the most salient one for a given user--item pair. Third, an SVD-based initialization seeds these slots with the KG's semantic geometry, so the model starts from a meaningful aspect space rather than from random noise. Without altering the KG itself, RA-GARK turns the KG's contribution from net-negative into net-positive. The value of this work is to show that the poor performance of KG-aware recommenders on sparse KGs is an architectural problem rather than an informational one: the same KG that is a liability under mandatory fusion becomes an asset once its use is made structurally optional. Graceful degradation thus offers a reusable principle for integrating any auxiliary signal whose reliability cannot be assumed in advance, including review-derived, cold-start, and privacy-constrained knowledge graphs.\\[0.5\baselineskip]
\textbf{Keywords: knowledge-graph-aware recommendation; graceful degradation; fusion gate; sparse knowledge graph; collaborative filtering.}
