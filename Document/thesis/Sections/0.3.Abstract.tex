    \textbf{Abstract} \\[0.3cm]

\end{center}

\normalsize

Knowledge-graph-aware (KG-aware) recommenders work well on dense KGs, but sparse KGs are underexplored. On a sparse review-derived KG ($2.4$ aspect edges per item), four representative methods (KGAT, KGCL, MCCLK, KGRec) are all outperformed by pure LightGCN: each embeds the KG into the scoring pipeline with no structural switch to disengage it when unreliable, letting KG noise contaminate the collaborative gradient.

We propose RA-GARK (Rationale-Aware Gating over Review-Aspect Knowledge graphs), which treats the KG as a side channel that can be switched on or off rather than a mandatory part of the scoring process, realized in three steps. First, each item's graph content is summarized into a few representative aspects, started from values that already carry its meaning rather than from random ones. Second, the model automatically picks the most relevant aspect for each user and item. Third, a fusion gate decides whether to open this channel at all: it starts almost closed, so training begins exactly like a pure LightGCN, and opens only when the channel actually helps, letting the model fall back gracefully when it is uninformative. Without altering the KG itself, RA-GARK turns the KG's contribution from net-negative into net-positive.

The value of this work is to show that the poor performance of KG-aware recommenders on sparse KGs is an architectural problem rather than an informational one: the same KG that is a liability under mandatory fusion becomes an asset once its use is made structurally optional. Graceful degradation thus offers a reusable principle for integrating any auxiliary signal whose reliability cannot be assumed in advance, including review-derived, cold-start, and privacy-constrained knowledge graphs.\\[0.5\baselineskip]
\textbf{Keywords: knowledge-graph-aware recommendation; graceful degradation; fusion gate; sparse knowledge graph; collaborative filtering.}
