    \textbf{Abstract} \\[0.5cm]

\end{center}

\normalsize 
  
Knowledge-graph-aware (KG-aware) recommenders improve accuracy when the KG is dense and clean, but the sparse or unreliable regime---typical of review-derived KGs, cold-start, and privacy-constrained settings---remains underexplored. On a sparse review-derived KG (on average $2.4$ aspect edges per item after filtering), four representative KG-aware methods (KGAT, KGCL, MCCLK, KGRec) are all outperformed by pure LightGCN. The shared cause is structural: each injects KG signal into the scoring path as a mandatory component, with no mechanism to disengage when the KG is unreliable, so KG noise contaminates the collaborative gradient flow.

This thesis proposes RA-GARK (Rationale-Aware Gating over Review-Aspect Knowledge graphs), a dual-view architecture that treats the KG as a gated side channel. Its core is a local-biased fusion gate that adjusts the relative weight of collaborative and KG signals per (user, item) pair; the gate's bias is initialized to $+5$, so training starts as effectively pure LightGCN and opens the KG channel only when gradients indicate it helps, giving a structural graceful-degradation guarantee. Each item's KG semantics are stored in $A=4$ latent aspect slots initialized by a truncated SVD of an IDF-weighted item--aspect co-occurrence matrix, and a softmax aspect-saliency attention selects the most salient slot per pair. On the sparse review-derived KG benchmark, RA-GARK reaches NDCG@20 $= 0.1238$, beating the strongest KG-aware baseline (KGRec) by $+13.1\%$ and pure LightGCN by $+5.0\%$---flipping the KG's contribution from net-negative to net-positive without changing the KG itself. Ablations: replacing softmax with sigmoid costs $7.0\%$ NDCG@20; removing the bias-initialized gate or the KG-SVD initialization each costs $5.3\%$. The attention-normalization observation is framed as a single-dataset hypothesis pending multi-dataset replication.
